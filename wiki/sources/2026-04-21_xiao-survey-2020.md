# [15] Xiao, Zhang, Lou & Hou — A Survey of Distributed Consensus Protocols for Blockchain Networks (2020)

**Category:** Survey · **Venue:** IEEE Communications Surveys & Tutorials
22(2), 2020.

## Full citation

Y. Xiao, N. Zhang, W. Lou, and Y. T. Hou, "A Survey of Distributed
Consensus Protocols for Blockchain Networks," *IEEE Communications
Surveys & Tutorials*, vol. 22, no. 2, pp. 1432–1465, 2020.

## Key takeaways

1. **Framing source for reported-metric ranges.** Provides aggregated
   throughput (tps), latency, fault-tolerance, and scalability ranges
   across Nakamoto, PoS, BFT-based, and hybrid consensus protocols. The
   thesis uses these ranges only as qualitative background for the
   comparative table in Ch. 2 — not as primary-source numbers, per the
   citation policy on [[concepts/annotated-bibliography]] §citation-policy.
2. **Pre-dates DAG-based family.** Coverage stops at 2019–2020:
   Narwhal+Tusk [11], Bullshark [12], and Mysticeti [13] are not in
   scope. Ch. 2's DAG-based section therefore cannot rely on [15] and
   must cite primary sources directly. Same coverage gap as [14] (Bano
   et al. SoK).
3. **Aggregation caveat.** The paper's quantitative ranges are
   aggregates across papers with inconsistent hardware, workloads, and
   batching assumptions — exactly the heterogeneous-harnesses problem
   called out on [[concepts/problem-statement]] §heterogeneous-harnesses.
   [15] is therefore cited as evidence that this gap exists, not as a
   source of accurate numbers.
4. **Taxonomy overlap with [14].** Uses a similar PoW / PoS /
   BFT-classical / hybrid decomposition as [14] but goes further into
   quantitative ranges; [14] stays qualitative. Both feed into
   [[concepts/consensus-families]] — [14] as the taxonomic spine, [15]
   as the quantitative-framing companion.
5. **Scalability discussion.** Covers validator-count scalability and
   message complexity across families in a way that informs — but does
   not substitute for — the scalability experiments T41–T44 on the
   simulator. Primary-source complexity claims (`O(n²)` for PBFT, `O(n)`
   for HotStuff, etc.) resolve to [4], [5].

## Limitations / gaps

Survey; no new experimental data. Quantitative ranges are aggregated
across inconsistent source measurements and should not be used as
primary-source performance claims. Pre-dates the DAG-based family and
the 2024-era Avalanche reanalysis [10]. Cited for framing only.
