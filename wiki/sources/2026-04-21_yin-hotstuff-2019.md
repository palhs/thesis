# [5] Yin, Malkhi, Reiter, Gueta & Abraham — HotStuff: BFT Consensus with Linearity and Responsiveness (2019)

**Category:** Protocol · **Venue:** PODC.

## Full citation

M. Yin, D. Malkhi, M. K. Reiter, G. G. Gueta, and I. Abraham, "HotStuff:
BFT Consensus with Linearity and Responsiveness," in *Proc. ACM Symp.
Principles of Distributed Computing (PODC)*, 2019, pp. 347–356.

## Key takeaways

1. **Linearises view change to `O(n)`.** The central result. Where PBFT
   [4] pays `O(n³)` in the worst-case view change and `O(n²)` on the
   normal path, HotStuff collapses both to `O(n)` via
   threshold-signature aggregation — every phase's quorum of `2f+1`
   votes is combined into a single `O(1)`-sized threshold signature that
   the next leader forwards.
2. **Responsiveness.** Progress rate is bounded only by actual network
   delay, not by pessimistic worst-case timers. This is what makes
   leader rotation cheap enough to be the *default* recovery mechanism
   rather than an exceptional one as in PBFT.
3. **Four-phase pipelined structure.** Each consensus instance runs
   prepare → pre-commit → commit → decide; consecutive instances are
   pipelined so each leader only does `O(n)` work per round. Basis of
   Diem/Libra's LibraBFT and the operational mental model for
   `O(n)`-message BFT. See [[algorithms/pbft]] §variants for the
   cross-comparison with Castro–Liskov and Tendermint.
4. **Still `3f+1`; still partial synchrony.** The quorum, fault, and
   synchrony assumptions are unchanged from [1], [3]. HotStuff optimises
   the *cost* of running the PBFT skeleton, not the underlying threshold.
5. **Optimisation baseline for the simulator.** The thesis's PBFT
   simulator module targets Castro–Liskov (task T28); HotStuff is
   descriptive context only — the `O(n)` vs `O(n²)` delta is interesting
   for plotting but not separately implemented (per
   [[algorithms/pbft]] §simulator-mapping).

## Limitations / gaps

Evaluated on largely synchronous conditions; limited adversarial-delay
modelling. Threshold-signature cryptographic cost (key setup, pairing
operations) is non-trivial in practice but abstracted out of the
simulator.
