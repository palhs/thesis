Act 3 · Results07 / 11

Results

"The results sit in four tabs, in the same order as the three run families."

▶Messages per committed unit at n = 25, log axis: Casper FFG ≈ 29 · PBFT ≈ 50 · Snowman ≈ 601.

▶Trends match theory — 1.15n · 2n · 2Kβ, per-validator: PBFT's 2n × n validators = the O(n²) from the diagram slide. FFG is cheapest because the epoch votes once — one attestation round per epoch against PBFT's two all-to-all phases per block — and that same epoch pacing is why its finality is ≈ 5 s, not 1 s: cheap and slow are one design choice. The order-of-magnitude gap is the price of subsampling.

reading layer, don't speak: latency flat in n — PBFT · SNW ≈ 1 s, FFG ≈ 5 s · goodput ≈ 95 · 95 · 80 tx/s · §4.2

▶Slowdown vs the zero-delay baseline: FFG ×1.3 · PBFT ×1.9. Both wait only for the fastest ⅔ — a quorum — so the slow stragglers never set the pace: the delay tail barely matters (the pair stays ≤ 3% apart across tail shapes).

▶Snowman ×12–15 — the opposite rule: each round it polls a random sample and must hear back from most of it, and it needs 15 successful rounds in a row — so it keeps running into the heavy tail, round after round. That is why its heavy-tail latency even exceeds its uniform-tail latency (15.3 s vs 12.6 s).

reading layer, don't speak: delay inflates time-to-finality, not message cost — msgs/unit PBFT ±0.1% · SNW +2% · FFG −12% · §4.3.1

▶Three finalization-rate curves across loss 0 → 20%.

▶Ranking PBFT > Snowman > FFG — PBFT alone still finalizes at 20%, because it alone has a recovery path: view-change rotates the leader. Snowman plateaus-then-cliffs at 10%; FFG collapses at the first 5% step — it has neither.

▶No protocol forks — loss consumes liveness (progress stops), not safety. "The central finding: the SAME design choice produces both each protocol's strength and its weakness."

▶ Snowman — K-peer subsampling thrives when peers are slow, starves when peers are silent — the sample finds no quorum to talk to. delay ×12–15 yet live · silence φ* = 0.10 / 0.20 · ε ≈ 5×10⁻¹⁵ / 3×10⁻¹¹
▶ PBFT — leader-quorum commit rides out delay, loss and silence — but past ⅓ equivocation it forks with no attribution. alive at 20% loss · delayed-voting success 1.0 · deterministic fork at φ = 0.40
▶ Casper FFG — epoch-paced finality cheapest, least delay-sensitive — first to collapse under loss; in exchange, the ONLY one with accountable safety. ≈ 1.15n msgs/unit · delay ×1.3 · collapses at 5% loss · ≥ ⅓ stake slashable

Act 4 · Closing10 / 11

Limitations & future work

Limitations

  • Simplified versions of each protocol
  • Networks of at most 25 validators
  • Loss = permanent drop, no retransmission — loss curves are worst-case bounds

Future work

  • Larger networks, with bandwidth & retransmission modeled
  • Signature aggregation (BLS / HotStuff-style)
  • A new protocol family implemented
The contribution is a mechanism map of the performance–security frontier — not the naming of a single winner.

The three contenders

1999

PBFT

quorum-BFT family
Castro & Liskov · MIT, OSDI '99
The first practical Byzantine fault-tolerant protocol: 3f+1 replicas, three voting phases, finality that is instant and deterministic.
in the wild Blueprint of the whole quorum line — its descendants run Cosmos (Tendermint) and Diem → Aptos (HotStuff).
2017

Casper FFG

PoS-finality family
Buterin & Griffith · Ethereum Foundation
A finality gadget: stake-weighted votes laid over a running chain finalise checkpoints epoch by epoch — misbehave and your stake is slashed.
in the wild The finality layer inside Gasper — securing Ethereum mainnet since the Merge (09/2022).
2019

Snowman

sampling family · Avalanche
"Team Rocket" · formalised at Cornell, built by Ava Labs
Consensus by repeated random polls: each node samples K peers until confidence builds; finality is probabilistic — 1 − ε.
in the wild The production engine of Avalanche — C-Chain & P-Chain since mainnet launch (2020).
One colour per protocol — amber, violet, ice — they tag every chart from here to the end.

PBFT view-change — the recovery path, live

Replicas time out → VIEW-CHANGE (itself all-to-all, quorum 2f+1 = 3 from {N1, N2, N3}) → NEW-VIEW: N1 becomes primary → the block replays. PBFT's structural recovery path — the reason it alone still finalizes at 20% loss (S8 · Tab B-Loss). Compare the three philosophies: S5 · Recovery ▸ Q&A chip.

Appendix · Q&A backupA4

Justified ≠ safe — the free escape, and the lock

Justified = one supermajority round — the next epoch may vote the same source to a later target (no double vote, no surround) and legally strand C. Finalization (⅔ on C → its direct child) closes exactly that exit: any later link that bypasses C must surround or double-vote → ≥⅓ stake slashable. That is the accountable-safety theorem — the "⅓ stake burn" on S5's spec strip.
